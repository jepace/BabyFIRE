babyFIRE FAQ

Is this actually feasible?

Yes, completely. The math is sound based on historical market performance. Even at conservative estimates (7% real return), a $127,303 deposit will grow to approximately $3.75M over 50 years, which supports $150,000/year under the 4% Safe Withdrawal Rate. Market returns have historically averaged 8-10%, so you may be even more conservative than necessary.

How do I prevent early withdrawals?

The primary tool is a trust. By working with an estate planning or elder law attorney, you can create a trust that:

  • Controls when and how money can be accessed
  • Restricts withdrawals to specific milestones (first home, age 30, etc.)
  • Prevents access before a certain age
  • Requires trustee approval for large withdrawals
  • Protects the money from creditors and poor financial decisions

Important: Consult a qualified attorney in your state—rules vary significantly by jurisdiction.

What are the tax implications?

This is complex and depends on your jurisdiction and how you structure the gift. Key considerations:

  • Gift tax: Gifts over the annual exclusion ($18,000 per person in 2024) may require gift tax returns
  • Financial aid: Assets in a minor's name can reduce eligibility for scholarships and grants
  • Kiddie tax: Unearned income on assets in a minor's name may be taxed at your rate above certain thresholds
  • State laws: Vary significantly; some states offer protective trust structures

Recommendation: Work with an estate planning attorney and a tax professional to structure this optimally for your situation.

Should I tell the beneficiary about the money?

This is one of the most important decisions you'll make. The consensus from financial professionals and experienced gift-givers:

  • Don't tell them until much later: Many recommend waiting until age 25-30 or even later
  • Why? Knowing about guaranteed retirement can reduce motivation to work hard, build resilience, and develop financial discipline
  • The risk: Generational wealth can lead to entitlement, lack of ambition, and poor life choices
  • The benefit: Letting them build their own career and life first, then revealing the gift later, preserves their drive and self-respect

Consider disclosing it when they're making major life decisions (home purchase, starting a family, etc.) rather than in their 20s.

What about education funding instead?

Many families choose to fund college through 529 plans instead, or in addition to retirement funding. Considerations:

  • College is more immediately useful: Eliminates student debt and career choice constraints in the 20s
  • Hybrid approach: Fund college through 529s, retire funding through a trust
  • Annual contributions: Smaller, regular gifts to parents can be more impactful now than a large inheritance later
  • Financial aid impact: 529s in a parent's name don't affect aid as severely as assets in the child's name
What if the money is needed for emergencies?

A well-designed trust can include flexibility for genuine hardship:

  • Medical emergencies
  • Business opportunities
  • Home down payment
  • Income disruption

The key is working with your attorney to define what constitutes a legitimate withdrawal vs. frivolous spending. Consider naming a trustee (yourself, a family member, or a professional) who can exercise discretion.

What about inflation?

This calculator already accounts for inflation through the "real rate of return" (investment return minus inflation rate).

  • Default: 10% return - 3% inflation = 7% real return
  • All income figures: Shown in "today's dollars" for purchasing power clarity
  • In 50 years: $150,000 in today's dollars will represent a much larger nominal amount, but with equivalent purchasing power

If you expect different inflation, adjust the "Expected Annual Inflation Rate" slider in the calculator.

What if I need the money later?

Money in a trust for a beneficiary is generally not available to you in retirement, but you have options:

  • Give yourself flexibility: Work with your attorney to ensure the trust doesn't over-commit money you might need
  • Annual exclusion gifts: Make annual tax-free gifts ($18,000/year per donor) rather than one large lump sum, preserving liquidity
  • Stagger contributions: Fund gradually over time rather than all at once
  • Document your intentions: Make sure your will and estate plan align with the trust
Should I tell other family members?

Privacy considerations:

  • Keep it quiet: The fewer people who know about the gift, the fewer complications arise
  • Tell the parents: Your children should know about gifts for their own children
  • Avoid comparison: If you're treating grandchildren differently, be mindful of family dynamics
  • Professional confidentiality: Your attorney and tax advisor should keep details private
What about grandkids of grandkids?

This is a consideration for generational wealth planning:

  • Dynasty trusts: Some states allow trusts that benefit multiple generations
  • Perpetual trusts: With proper planning, wealth can be set aside for great-grandchildren
  • Practical reality: Generational wealth often dissipates by the third generation due to consumption and poor decision-making
  • Focus on values: More important than creating a dynasty is instilling financial discipline and values across generations
How do I get started?

Step 1: Use this calculator to determine your target deposit amount

Step 2: Consult with an estate planning attorney in your state about trust structures and tax implications

Step 3: Talk to a financial advisor about investment strategy and account types

Step 4: Have conversations with your children about your intentions and values

Step 5: Set up the accounts/trusts and invest in a diversified, low-cost index fund portfolio

Step 6: Review periodically and adjust as needed
